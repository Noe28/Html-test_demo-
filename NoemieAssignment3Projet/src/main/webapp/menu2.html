A bit of History


October 24, 2022 | Comments

Yesterday at the centre of the world, man will now be no more than a stardust among billions of galaxies.

Most historians attribute the invention of the telescope to Hans Lip-pershey, a Dutch spectacle maker. In 1608, he combined a convex and a concave lens to create the first telescope. The discovery of the telescope marks a fundamental step in human history. The celestial observations made possible by this instrument completely changed the way scientists had previously viewed the universe.

On 2 October 1608, a Dutch optician, Hans Lippershey, filed a patent for a device that made it possible to "see distant things as if they were near". To do this, he placed two lenses in a tube, one at each end. The production of these spectacles was carried out in great numbers and the device was sold in Paris in April 1609. If the invention proved indispensable to the military for obvious reasons, it obviously did not take long for it to be turned towards the sky.


Since the dawn of humanity, we have been observing the sky and trying to understand it. In the beginning we only had our eyes, but four centuries ago an instrument was born that revolutionised astronomy. This instrument consisted of lenses, pieces of glass or cut rock crystal whose properties had been known since antiquity.

The following year, Galileo, the Italian inventor, was the first to point a telescope of his own making at the heavens. His observations enabled him to discover the satellites of Jupiter, the mountains of the Moon, the phases of Venus, the spots on the Sun and countless stars invisible to the naked eye. After restating it, he sweeps aside the thesis of the imutability of the cosmos, formulated in antiquity by Aristotle. And, above all, he validates the Copernican theory, which holds that the Earth turn around the Sun and not the other way around.

In parallel with his remarkable work in optics, the English physicist Isaac Newton developed a telescope in 1671 that revolutionised astronomical observation: the reflecting telescope. It consists of a concave primary mirror placed at the bottom of an open tube. An inverted image is thus observed at the bottom of the eyepiece. After Newton, the German William Herschel (1738-1822) observed numerous clusters and nebulae and studied the structure of the Milky Way, and the Irish Lord Rosse (1800-1867) discovered the spiral shapes of certain objects.


From then on, modern astronomy, which in the 20th century led to the discovery of the Big Bang (the original explosion that created a continuously expanding universe), took off thanks to the telescope.
