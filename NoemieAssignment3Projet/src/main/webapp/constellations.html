What is a constellation? It is simply a grouping of stars which, in ancient times, were linked to mythical figures such as gods and legendary heroes. Constellations help us to delineate the sky in well-defined areas, in short, to create a map. This makes it easier to locate a particular object, planet or star in deep space. Amateur astronomers today use a mixture of ancient and modern constellations.

Names and denominations of stars

There are bright stars that have been called by various names by different civilisations. The names that have survived to the present day are mainly due to the Arabs and Greeks. Some examples of the most popular stars are Sirius in the constellation Can Mayor, Polaris or the North Star in the constellation Ursa Minor, or Alnilam, Alnitak and Mintaka which are the three stars that form the belt of Orion (also called the "3 kings").


For this reason, there are star naming systems that allow us to name and identify stars more easily. One of the most popular and widely used among amateur astronomers is the one created by the German astronomer Johannes Bayer. It consists of the following: it ranks the stars in a constellation from brightest to darkest using the names of the letters of the Greek alphabet.


For example, the brightest is called alpha, followed by beta, then gamma, and so on. He then adds the Latin genitive according to the constellation to which it belongs. For example, the brightest star in the constellation Sagittarius would be Alpha Sagitarii, the second brightest star in the constellation Orion would be Beta Orioni, etc. This makes it easier to name and refer to the stars in a constellation.
